The Ruler as a Tiling

Both rulers have a second life as a picture. Run the diagonal of an S×S box and, at every integer step k, hang a block down-and-left with its NE corner pinned to the diagonal. Size the block by the priority of k — its ruler value — and the whole lower-left triangle tiles perfectly, one color per priority.

Two number systems, two block shapes

The shape of the blocks is exactly the difference between the two systems:

Dyadic. A priority-n block is a 2ⁿ × 2ⁿ square — because 2ⁿ⁺¹ = 2ⁿ + 2ⁿ, the two halves are equal, so the triangle splits into a square plus two identical half-triangles. That equal split is the binary doubling that makes the dyadic map self-similar in powers of two.

Fibonacci. A priority-n block is an Fₙ × Fₙ₋₁ rectangle — because Fₙ₊₁ = Fₙ + Fₙ₋₁ with the two parts unequal, so the triangle splits into a rectangle plus a larger sub-triangle (Fₙ) and a smaller one (Fₙ₋₁). The Fibonacci recurrence is the recursion of the figure.

Dyadic — 2ⁿ squares

Fibonacci — Fₙ × Fₙ₋₁ rectangles

Slide n to grow the maximum sum. Each new level adds its own color and, with it, the next self-similar shell of the tiling — the geometric face of swapping the priority function from base two to base φ.
